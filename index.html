Reading the weather and the calendar…
How this is calculated. The plant-date yield curve blends two sources, weighted 65% / 35%. The primary source is the published University of Illinois Nafziger trials — 22 site-years of corn (2007–2021) and 26 site-years of soybeans (2010–2020), reported in Farmdoc Daily. Anchor benchmarks: May 10 ≈ 95%, May 20 ≈ 91%, May 30 ≈ 86% of maximum yield, with peak in mid-to-late April. The secondary source is a local 1,300+ field central-Illinois dataset (2023–2025), which sharpens the recent shape of the curve, especially the corn plateau through mid-May. The published trials get the larger weight because they cover 20+ years of weather variability — far longer than the three-year local sample. Soil-temperature thresholds and imbibitional-chilling guidance follow Iowa State, UNL CropWatch, and Peterson Ag agronomy bulletins. GDU uses base 50 °F / cap 86 °F. The 10-year normal GDU line is an average of the previous ten years' GDU accumulation at this exact lat/lon (Open-Meteo archive). The decision logic blends three things: where you are on the yield-potential calendar, whether the next 48 hours protect the seed, and how fast the heat will pull the crop out of the ground.

Field conditions are not factored into the recommendation. Weather-based models can't reliably predict whether a particular field is fit to plant — soil type, drainage, tile, residue cover, slope, and recent foot/tire traffic all matter, and none of that is visible in a forecast. Walk your fields and trust your boots; this tool tells you whether the agronomic and calendar windows are open, not whether the dirt is ready.
